**Best Practices for Mean Reversion Strategies**

Mean reversion strategies look to capitalize on extreme moves and temporary price dislocations. However, because they inherently carry negative skew (often characterized as "up up up big drop"), they require careful implementation and strict discipline. Experts like Rob Hanna, Rob Carver, and David Bush highlight several key best practices:

*   **Establish a Research-First Edge:** Before building a system, analyze historical market behaviors (e.g., "what happens after a 5-day low in an uptrend?") to find robust, repeatable edges. Avoid the "rookie" approach of slapping standard indicators together and curve-fitting them to remove losers. 
*   **Apply Regime and Market Bias Filters:** Broad market conditions should dictate your strategy. Rob Hanna uses a composite "Aggregator" to measure short-term market bias. He only deploys long mean-reversion models when the broad market is bullish and isn't overbought. When the broad market turns bearish, he stops taking new long trades and activates short models instead.
*   **Select Your Universe Carefully:** **Avoid small caps.** The lack of institutional sponsorship in small caps makes them prone to structural problems and "falling knife" behavior. It is much safer to restrict your universe to highly liquid large caps, such as S&P 500 or Nasdaq-100 stocks.
*   **Pair with Momentum Strategies:** Mean reversion works brilliantly as a stabilizing force in a diversified portfolio. For example, short-term mean-reversion short strategies can help rein in open exposure when long momentum positions become overheated. Conversely, long mean-reversion models can catch sharp bounces, offsetting the drag of short momentum trades. 

**How to Distribute Entries and Exits**

Because mean reversion frequently involves buying into heavy selling, **position sizing is the most critical risk management technique**. Here is how the experts distribute entries, exits, and capital to survive the volatility:

*   **Scale In Gradually:** Do not make your maximum bet on the first signal. Use a scaling-in approach to smooth out your entry prices as selling intensifies, which prevents you from taking oversized losses if a market temporarily overshoots your signal. Rob Carver applies "continuous forecasting" to scale his trades, using quantitative values (e.g., +10 for average bullishness) rather than binary "all-in or flat" decisions. This continuous method allows for gradual position building and reduction, leading to a much smoother risk profile.
*   **Throttle Daily Exposure:** You must avoid "loading the boat" on a single ugly market day. You can throttle your daily exposure by using multiple different models to spread out your entries over time. 
*   **Use Strict Caps and Sector Limits:** Set hard limits on your risk distribution. For example, fix a fractional position size per signal, limit yourself to *N* new names per day, and cap your total active portfolio at 10 to 20 positions. Furthermore, cap exposure to any single sector at a strict maximum (like 30%) to avoid getting destroyed by sector-specific crashes, such as a financial sector pile-up. 
*   **Run Complementary Long and Short Engines:** Distribute your trading opportunities across both sides of the market. Hanna uses a system called "Catapult" to scale into oversold long positions, while simultaneously running a short mean-reversion system called "Icarus" to fade over-stretched high-flyers. Together, these complementary engines ensure that when the long strategy struggles, the short strategy can often generate returns.
*   **Use Non-Overlapping Sub-Universes:** To keep position sizing in check, divide your tradable universe into distinct groups. For example, you can build one model for a specific set of 100 stocks and another for the remaining 400, ensuring a stock belongs to *only one* model to prevent doubled-up exposure. 
*   **Beware of Left-Tail Risk:** While scaling into a dip is a core mechanic, you must not cross the line into "averaging into stupidity." Mean reversion trades can explode, so explicit rules for cutting risk and stepping aside during stressed regimes are non-negotiable.
